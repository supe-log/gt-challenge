# BrainLift: The CogAT Is Enough

**Owner:** Arvind Nagarajan
**Purpose:** Steel-man the opposite case — that the CogAT should remain the primary fixed hurdle for GT school entry, and there is no compelling reason to replace it.
**Version:** 1.0 | 2026-03-28
**Status:** Adversarial research document — built to pressure-test GT Challenge's positioning

---

## Purpose

This BrainLift argues the spiky POV: **"The CogAT is a sufficient, validated, cost-effective, and equitable gifted identification instrument. Replacing it with unvalidated alternatives introduces more risk than it solves."**

This is the case that every school psychologist, district GT coordinator, and test publisher would make against GT Challenge. If we can't defeat this argument, we shouldn't build GT Challenge. If we can defeat it, the specifics of HOW we defeat it will make our own BrainLift dramatically stronger.

## DOK1: Fact Base — The Case for the CogAT

### Category 1: CogAT Psychometric Properties

**Source 1: CogAT Form 7/8 Technical Manual (Lohman & Hagen)**
- Publisher: Riverside Insights. Grades K-12. Group administration, 90-120 minutes.
- Three batteries: Verbal, Quantitative, Nonverbal.
- Composite reliability: α = 0.95-0.97. Individual batteries: α = 0.90-0.95.
- Split-half reliability: .904 for subtests, .927 for total score.
- Cronbach's alpha: .941 for subtests, .962 for total score.
- Form 7 standardization sample: **65,350 students** from American K-12 schools, all geographic regions, all ethnic groups, EL and IEP students included proportionally.
- Form 8: completely new items, zero content overlap with Form 7, norms updated to 2017.
- Development: 9+ years, 20,000+ pilot items, four forms, two doctoral dissertations, 10+ research publications.

**Source 2: Ozen, Pereira, Karatas, Castillo-Hermosilla, & Maeda (2025) — CogAT Meta-Analysis**
- Published in *Gifted Child Quarterly*, 2025.
- Average correlation between CogAT and other instruments: **r = .63, 95% CI [.57, .69]**.
- For comparison, NNAT's average correlation with other instruments: r = .44 (Lee et al., 2021).
- CogAT's multi-battery structure captures substantially more of the construct space than single-construct tests.

**Source 3: CogAT Predictive Validity (Lohman, 2005)**
- CogAT Composite correlated with ITBS Reading Total at **r = .79** (co-normed sample).
- Grade 4 CogAT Verbal was the best predictor of Grade 9 Reading achievement.
- Grade 4 CogAT Quantitative was the best predictor of Grade 9 Mathematics achievement.
- Published: Lohman, D. F. (2005). "Gifted today but not tomorrow? Longitudinal changes in ability and achievement during elementary school." *Journal for the Education of the Gifted*, 29, 451-484.

**Source 4: CogAT Cost-Effectiveness**
- Group administration: ~$5-15 per student at district scale.
- Individual private administration (university testing centers): $89-$180 per student.
- Compare: WISC-V private administration: $1,500-3,000+ (psychologist required).
- CogAT can screen an entire grade level for the cost of one WISC-V evaluation.

### Category 2: g Factor as the Gold Standard Predictor

**Source 5: Schmidt & Hunter (1998) — 85-Year Meta-Analysis**
- "The Validity and Utility of Selection Methods in Personnel Psychology." *Psychological Bulletin*, 124(2), 262-274.
- General mental ability (GMA/g) predicting job performance: **r = .51** (medium-complexity jobs).
- GMA predicting training success: **r = .56**.
- By comparison: conscientiousness r = .31; integrity tests r = .41; education r = .10; job experience r = .18.
- GMA is the single strongest predictor of performance across 85 years of research. No other single construct comes close.

**Source 6: Gottfredson (1997) — "Why g Matters"**
- *Intelligence*, 24(1), 79-132.
- g is the ability to deal with cognitive complexity in everyday life.
- The ratio of SD in job performance to mean performance: 19% (low-complexity), 32% (medium), **48% (high-complexity jobs)** — g matters more as complexity increases.
- Gifted education IS high-complexity education. Therefore g should be the primary selection criterion.

**Source 7: Kuncel, Hezlett, & Ones (2001/2004) — GRE Meta-Analysis**
- 1,753 independent samples, 82,659 graduate students.
- GRE-V predicting graduate GPA: rho = .34. GRE-Q: rho = .32.
- GRE scores predict doctoral attainment, publication counts, faculty ratings.
- Kuncel & Hezlett (2010) debunked five myths about ability tests: no bias, no threshold effects, not redundant with SES, predicts creativity/leadership.

**Source 8: Deary, Whalley, & Starr — Scottish Longitudinal Studies**
- Childhood IQ at age 11 correlated with IQ at age 77: **r = .66-.73**.
- One-SD advantage in childhood IQ associated with ~20% lower mortality risk.
- Childhood IQ predicted educational attainment, occupation, income, and health over 60+ years.
- Published across multiple papers: Deary et al. (2004) in *Intelligence*; Deary (2012) in *Current Directions in Psychological Science*.

**Source 9: Lubinski & Benbow — SMPY 35-Year Follow-Up**
- Study of Mathematically Precocious Youth, tracking gifted adolescents identified by SAT at age 12-13.
- Published: Lubinski & Benbow (2006), "Study of Mathematically Precocious Youth After 35 Years," *Perspectives on Psychological Science*, 1(4), 316-345.
- Even within the top 1% of ability, further differentiation by cognitive level predicted differential outcomes.
- Top 0.01% earned more doctorates, patents, publications, tenure at top universities than those "merely" in the top 1%.
- **SAT scores at age 13 predicted creative-productive achievement at ages 33-48.**
- This directly refutes the claim that g doesn't predict "real-world outcomes" or "creative-productive achievement."

### Category 3: The Case Against Grit/Appetite as Identification Criteria

**Source 10: Credé, Tynan, & Harms (2017) — Grit Meta-Analysis**
- "Much Ado About Grit." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 113(3), 492-511.
- 584 effect sizes, 88 independent samples, 66,807 individuals.
- Grit predicting GPA: **rho = .18**.
- Grit-conscientiousness correlation: **rho = .84** — grit is essentially conscientiousness relabeled.
- **Incremental validity of grit beyond conscientiousness: approximately zero** (ΔR² ≈ .01).
- Grit explains **~2% of academic variance**. Cognitive ability explains **~25%+** (r = .51² = .26).
- The ratio: cognitive ability explains **12.5x more variance** than grit.

**Source 11: Rimfeld, Kovas, Dale, & Plomin (2016) — Twin Study**
- "True Grit and Genetics." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 111(5), 780-789.
- 4,642 participants (2,321 twin pairs), TEDS sample, age 16.
- Grit Perseverance heritability: **37%**. Consistency of Interest heritability: **20%**.
- Grit Perseverance-Conscientiousness genetic correlation: **.86** — genetically, they are the same construct.
- Big Five personality predicts 5.6% of GCSE variance. Adding grit increases explained variance by only **0.5%**.
- **88% of Grit Perseverance's correlation with academic achievement was genetically mediated.**
- Grit is not a learnable skill that schools can develop — it is largely genetic, like the cognitive ability it claims to supplement.

### Category 4: The Case Against Multi-Session/Retesting

**Source 12: Lievens, Reeve, & Heggestad (2007) — Retesting Destroys Validity**
- "An examination of psychometric bias due to retesting." *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 92(6), 1672-1682.
- 941 candidates tested and retested.
- Initial test criterion validity: **r = .19**. Retest criterion validity: **r = .00**.
- Retest scores were **less g-saturated and more memory-dependent**.
- Retesting did not improve measurement — it degraded it. The retest measured memory of the first test, not cognitive ability.

**Source 13: Scharfen, Peters, & Holling (2018) — Retest Effects Meta-Analysis**
- Published in *Intelligence*.
- Retesting produces approximately **0.67 SD gain** (~10 IQ points) across repeated administrations.
- First retest: **~4.26 IQ point gain** on average.
- These gains are practice effects, not ability gains — they inflate scores without improving the construct being measured.

**Source 14: Regression to the Mean**
- Students scoring at the extremes on Test 1 will regress toward the mean on Test 2 due to measurement error.
- A child who scored 135 on Day 1 (true score 130 + 5 points positive error) will likely score closer to 130 on Day 2.
- Multi-session "rolling estimates" will systematically pull extreme scorers toward the center, making it HARDER to identify the truly gifted, not easier.
- This is a mathematical certainty, not a debatable claim.

**Source 15: Attrition Bias in Multi-Session Designs**
- Multi-session designs lose participants between sessions.
- Who drops out? Children from unstable households, lower-SES families, families without consistent device access, children with less parental oversight.
- Who returns? Children with motivated, engaged, higher-SES parents who ensure their child completes multiple sessions.
- **Multi-session testing introduces the exact same SES confound it claims to eliminate** — but through differential attrition rather than differential coaching.
- The attrition is invisible in the data (you only see who returned, not who didn't), making it harder to detect and correct than coaching effects.

### Category 5: The Dangers of Unvalidated Instruments

**Source 16: Lohman (2005) on Nonverbal-Only Tests**
- "The role of nonverbal ability tests in identifying academically gifted students." *Gifted Child Quarterly*, 49(2), 111-138.
- The CogAT Nonverbal Battery alone identified **only 18% of the best readers**.
- Selecting gifted students solely on nonverbal reasoning would **exclude the majority of the most academically accomplished students in all ethnic groups**.
- Lohman: "I am dismayed by the claims that have been made for such tests... children are being hurt by selection policies that use nonverbal reasoning tests as the criteria of first resort."
- **Lesson: well-intentioned alternative assessments can HARM the children they intend to help.**

**Source 17: Card & Giuliano (2016) — Universal Screening WITH CogAT Works**
- When a large Florida district moved from teacher referral to **universal CogAT screening**:
  - Hispanic gifted identification: **+130%**
  - Black gifted identification: **+80%**
  - Free/reduced lunch: **+180%**
- The equity problem was the **referral system**, not the CogAT itself.
- Universal CogAT screening is the most evidence-backed equity intervention in gifted education.

**Source 18: NYC's Failed Alternatives**
- In 2021-2022, NYC replaced standardized testing with teacher recommendations for gifted admissions.
- Result: no transparency, no auditable data, concerns about racial bias in teacher nominations.
- The Adams administration reversed the phase-out — the alternative was worse than the test.
- NYC demonstrates what happens when validated instruments are replaced with unvalidated alternatives: you lose the ability to audit, calibrate, and improve.

### Category 6: Coaching Effects Are Manageable

**Source 19: Te Nijenhuis et al. (2007) — Coaching Gains NOT on g**
- Coaching gains on IQ/cognitive tests average 5-7 IQ points.
- These gains are **NOT on the g factor** — they represent test-specific skills, not genuine cognitive improvement.
- **This means the CogAT's g measurement is ROBUST to coaching.** Coached children may score a few points higher on surface features, but their underlying g estimate remains approximately accurate.
- The coached score is noisy but the signal is preserved.

**Source 20: Lohman on Coaching as Familiarization**
- Lohman estimated focused CogAT prep could raise scores 5-10 percentile points.
- He argued that some familiarization is actually **desirable** — a child who has never seen a matrix analogy may score artificially low on first exposure.
- Brief familiarization (seeing the format once) accounts for most of the coaching effect (+2-5 IQ points).
- The remaining coaching effect (intensive prep) is modest and doesn't transfer to different tests.

**Source 21: Briggs (2004) — Coaching Effects Are Small**
- SAT coaching effects: 0.05-0.15 SD verbal, 0.15-0.25 SD quantitative.
- DerSimonian & Laird: positive coaching effects are "too small to be practically important."
- The same pattern applies to cognitive ability tests: coaching effects exist but are modest relative to genuine ability differences.

### Category 7: Dynamic Assessment Is Not Ready

**Source 22: Grigorenko & Sternberg (1998) — Dynamic Testing Review**
- "Dynamic Testing." *Psychological Bulletin*, 124(1), 75-111.
- Test-retest reliability of dynamic assessment is often **not reported or is low**.
- Scoring is **heavily subjective**, depending on examiner-examinee interaction.
- Standardization across examiners is **extremely difficult**.
- Predictive validity evidence is **thin compared to static tests**.

**Source 23: Caffrey, Fuchs, & Fuchs (2008) — Dynamic Assessment Meta-Review**
- "The Predictive Validity of Dynamic Assessment." *Journal of Special Education*, 41(4), 254-270.
- Dynamic assessment showed some predictive validity but was **not consistently superior to static testing**.
- Substantial heterogeneity in results across studies.
- Dynamic assessment may simply be a less reliable, more expensive way of measuring the same construct (g).

**Source 24: Behavioral Rating Unreliability for Children**
- Achenbach, McConaughy, & Howell (1987) meta-analysis: cross-informant correlations for child behavioral ratings average **r = .28** between parents and teachers.
- Child self-reports are even less stable, especially below age 10-12.
- Any "behavioral appetite signal" measured through platform interaction is a behavioral rating — subject to the same reliability concerns.

## DOK2: What the Fact Base Tells Us (Synthesized)

### Synthesis 1: The CogAT already solves the problems it's accused of having.

The standard critique of the CogAT is: it's coachable, it's a single session, it's a single score. But the DOK1 evidence shows each critique is overstated:

- **Coachable?** Yes, by 5-10 percentile points. But coaching gains are NOT on g (Te Nijenhuis, Source 19). The CogAT still measures what it claims to measure. The coaching effect is noise, not bias. Compare to the "appetite signal" alternative: grit explains 2% of variance (Source 10) vs. g's 25%+. Replacing a noisy-but-valid measure with a clean-but-weak measure is a downgrade.

- **Single session?** Yes. But retesting DEGRADES validity — retest scores are less g-saturated and have zero criterion validity (Source 12). The single session is not a bug; it is the design that preserves construct validity. Multi-session testing measures memory and motivation confounds, not cleaner ability estimates.

- **Single score?** No. The CogAT produces three battery scores plus a composite, plus ability profiles. Lohman's profile analysis reveals specific cognitive strengths/weaknesses invisible to single-score instruments. The CogAT is already multi-dimensional.

### Synthesis 2: The equity case for replacing the CogAT is backwards.

The evidence shows:
- Universal CogAT screening **increased** minority identification by 80-180% (Source 17).
- The equity problem is referral bias, not test bias.
- NYC's attempt to replace testing with alternatives **reduced** transparency and auditability (Source 18).
- Multi-session testing introduces **attrition bias** that correlates with SES (Source 15).

The CogAT combined with universal screening is the most evidence-backed equity intervention available. Replacing it with an unvalidated multi-session behavioral platform risks reintroducing the very inequities it claims to solve — just through a different mechanism (attrition instead of coaching).

### Synthesis 3: "Appetite" is not a viable identification construct.

The DOK1 evidence is devastating:
- Grit predicts 2% of academic variance; cognitive ability predicts 25%+ (Sources 10, 5).
- Grit is 84% overlapping with conscientiousness — it's not a new construct (Source 10).
- Grit is 37-48% heritable — it's not a learnable skill (Source 11).
- 88% of grit's correlation with achievement is genetically mediated (Source 11).
- Behavioral ratings of children have cross-informant reliability of r = .28 (Source 24).

An identification system that weights "appetite" (a construct with 2% explained variance and ~.28 measurement reliability) alongside "aptitude" (a construct with 25%+ explained variance and .95+ reliability) is not more rigorous. It's diluting signal with noise.

### Synthesis 4: The CogAT's psychometric infrastructure is irreplaceable.

- 9+ years of development, 65,350-student norming sample, 20,000+ pilot items (Source 1).
- Meta-analytic validity: r = .63 against other instruments (Source 2).
- Longitudinal predictive validity: Grade 4 CogAT predicts Grade 9 achievement (Source 3).
- Cost: $5-15/student vs. $1,500-3,000 for individual tests (Source 4).

Building a replacement with equivalent psychometric rigor would require years of development, hundreds of thousands of calibration responses, and independent validation studies. The CogAT represents decades of accumulated psychometric capital. The claim that a startup's unvalidated adaptive platform can replace this is not merely premature — it is irresponsible.

### Synthesis 5: g works. The SMPY data proves it.

The Lubinski & Benbow SMPY data (Source 9) is the single most important finding in this debate. It shows:
- SAT scores at age 13 predict creative-productive achievement at ages 33-48.
- Even within the top 1%, further cognitive differentiation predicts differential outcomes.
- The top 0.01% dramatically outperform the top 1% on patents, publications, doctorates, and income.

This directly refutes the GT Challenge BrainLift's SPOV that "IQ tests don't measure intelligence in any way that matters for identifying future achievers." The SMPY data says the opposite: g measured in adolescence is the **single best long-term predictor** of exactly the creative-productive achievement that gifted programs aim to develop.

## DOK3: Strategic Insights — Crossing Domains

### Insight 1: The CogAT's greatest vulnerability is access, not validity — and universal screening already fixes this.

**Built from Synthesis 1 (CogAT solves its own problems) + Synthesis 2 (equity case is backwards).**

The GT Challenge BrainLift correctly identifies that gifted testing has an equity problem. But it misdiagnoses the cause. The problem is not the CogAT instrument — it is the referral and access system surrounding it. Card & Giuliano (2016) proved this definitively: when you screen every child with the CogAT, minority identification increases by 80-180%.

The policy intervention is simple: mandate universal CogAT screening in every district. This costs $5-15 per student (far less than building an alternative platform), uses a validated instrument with 65,350-student norms and r = .63 meta-analytic validity, and has the strongest equity evidence of any intervention in the field.

GT Challenge is solving a problem that universal screening already solves, but with an unvalidated instrument that introduces new problems (attrition bias, behavioral measurement unreliability, practice effects).

### Insight 2: Multi-session testing is not a feature — it is a psychometric liability.

**Built from Synthesis 1 (single session preserves validity) + DOK1 Sources 12-15.**

The GT Challenge BrainLift claims multi-session testing is "categorically superior" to single-session testing. The psychometric evidence says the opposite:

- Retesting degrades g-saturation (Lievens et al., 2007): retest criterion validity drops from r = .19 to r = .00.
- Retesting inflates scores by ~0.67 SD (Scharfen et al., 2018) through practice effects, not ability gains.
- Multi-session designs introduce attrition bias that systematically favors higher-SES families.
- Regression to the mean pulls extreme scorers toward the center, making gifted identification HARDER.

The single-session format is not a limitation of the CogAT — it is a design choice that preserves construct validity by avoiding practice effects, memory confounds, and attrition bias. The GT Challenge BrainLift never addresses any of these mechanisms. It asserts "more sessions = better" without engaging with the psychometric evidence that more sessions = noisier.

### Insight 3: "Coaching = learning" is an unfalsifiable claim that makes the test uninterpretable.

**Built from Synthesis 3 (appetite is not viable) + DOK1 Source 19 (Te Nijenhuis).**

The GT Challenge BrainLift's core philosophical move is: "coaching gains on our platform are learning, not inflation." This sounds appealing but creates a fatal psychometric problem: **if all score improvement is "learning," then the test cannot distinguish genuine ability from practiced performance.**

On the CogAT, we know that coaching gains are NOT on g (Te Nijenhuis). This means the g signal is preserved even in the presence of coaching. The test still measures what it claims to measure.

On GT Challenge, by design, coaching gains ARE the signal. But this means there is no way to separate:
- A child with high ability who practiced a lot → high score
- A child with moderate ability whose parent drilled them for 100 hours → high score
- A child with low ability and very high drive who practiced 200 hours → high score

All three score the same, and the platform calls all three "gifted." But gifted programs are designed for children who need advanced instruction because they learn faster — not for children who practiced a lot. A child who achieved a high score through 200 hours of drilling may not benefit from, or keep up with, an accelerated curriculum.

By embracing coaching, GT Challenge sacrifices the ability to interpret what its scores mean. The CogAT, by being "gameable but robust at the construct level," preserves interpretability.

### Insight 4: Behavioral "appetite signals" are confounded by everything except appetite.

**Built from Synthesis 3 + DOK1 Sources 15, 24.**

GT Challenge measures "appetite" through: return visits, persistence, voluntary hard items, learning velocity, time investment, streak. The claim is these measure intrinsic learning drive. But each signal has confounds the BrainLift never addresses:

| Signal | What it claims to measure | What it actually measures |
|--------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| Return visits | Consistency of interest | Parental pressure, device access, schedule flexibility |
| Persistence | Perseverance of effort | Parental hovering, not understanding the quit option, frustration tolerance (which is age- and mood-dependent) |
| Voluntary hard items | Need for cognition | Misunderstanding the interface, parental coaching to "choose the hard one" |
| Learning velocity | Zone of Proximal Development | Familiarity with item types, prior exposure to content |
| Time investment | Task commitment | Nothing to do that afternoon, parent-enforced screen time |
| Streak | Habit formation | Parent sets daily reminder, household routine |

Cross-informant reliability for child behavioral ratings is r = .28 (Achenbach et al., 1987). There is no reason to believe that machine-observed behavioral signals will be more reliable than human-observed ones — the confounds are different but equally severe.

### Insight 5: The CogAT as a fixed hurdle is the most equitable architecture — because it is the most transparent.

**Built from Synthesis 2 (equity case is backwards) + Synthesis 4 (irreplaceable psychometric infrastructure).**

A fixed hurdle has a critical equity property: **everyone knows exactly what the bar is.** The CogAT cutoff (e.g., 97th percentile on the composite) is:
- Public and auditable
- The same for every child
- Measurable by a validated instrument with known properties
- Independent of parental behavior, device access, or household routine

A composite score that blends aptitude theta with behavioral appetite signals, learning velocity, persistence metrics, and proctoring validation is:
- Opaque (which signals matter? how are they weighted?)
- Variable (children in different home environments generate different behavioral data)
- Confounded (see Insight 4)
- Unvalidated (no norming sample, no predictive validity data, no independent replication)

For a parent — especially a low-income, non-English-speaking parent — a transparent fixed hurdle ("score above X on this test") is more equitable than a complex composite they cannot understand, influence, or challenge. The CogAT's simplicity is not a weakness. It is an equity feature.

## DOK4: The Spiky POV — Keep the CogAT

### SPOV: The CogAT, universally administered, is the most defensible gifted identification system available — and replacing it with unvalidated alternatives is more likely to harm children than help them.

**Derived from:** All five DOK3 insights converging.

**The argument:**

The case against the CogAT rests on three pillars: it's coachable, it's a single session, and it misses "appetite." All three pillars are weaker than they appear:

**Pillar 1 — Coachability:** Coaching gains are 5-10 percentile points (Lohman) and are NOT on g (Te Nijenhuis). The CogAT's three-battery structure (Verbal + Quantitative + Nonverbal) dilutes coaching effects across domains. A parent who coaches all three batteries is, in Lohman's terms, providing "good educational practice" — and the resulting score improvement may partly reflect genuine ability development. The solution to coaching is not to build a "gameable" test — it is to ensure universal access so coaching advantages are shared, not monopolized.

**Pillar 2 — Single session:** Multi-session testing sounds better but is psychometrically worse. Retesting degrades g-saturation (Lievens et al., r drops from .19 to .00). Practice effects inflate scores by 0.67 SD (Scharfen et al.). Attrition bias favors higher-SES families. Regression to the mean punishes the truly extreme. The single session is the design that maximizes construct validity. If you want higher reliability, give the WISC-V to borderline cases — don't retest everyone.

**Pillar 3 — Missing appetite:** Grit/appetite explains 2% of academic variance. Cognitive ability explains 25%+. The ratio is 12.5:1 in favor of cognition. Grit is 84% overlapping with conscientiousness and 37-48% heritable. Behavioral signals in children have cross-informant reliability of r = .28. The "appetite measurement" innovation is not measuring a powerful missing construct — it is measuring a weak, genetically-loaded, unreliable signal and weighting it alongside the single strongest predictor in all of psychology.

**The affirmative case:**

The CogAT is:
1. **Validated**: 65,350-student norms, r = .63 meta-analytic validity, 9+ years of development.
2. **Predictive**: CogAT Composite-ITBS Reading r = .79. Grade 4 CogAT predicts Grade 9 achievement.
3. **Equitable when universally administered**: Card & Giuliano showed +80-180% minority identification with universal CogAT screening.
4. **Robust to coaching at the construct level**: Te Nijenhuis showed coaching doesn't move g.
5. **Cost-effective**: $5-15/student vs. $1,500-3,000 for individual tests.
6. **Transparent**: Fixed cutoff, same for everyone, auditable, challengeable.

The reform the field needs is not a new test — it is **universal administration of the existing test**. Fund universal CogAT screening in every district. That costs approximately $5-15 per student. The equity gains are proven. The psychometric validity is established. The risk is near zero.

Building an unvalidated adaptive platform with unvalidated behavioral metrics and calling it a replacement is the kind of well-intentioned innovation that, per Lohman (2005), ends up "hurting the children it intends to help."

**Conviction: 8/10.** The evidence is strong. The main weakness of this position is that it accepts the status quo — but the status quo, properly implemented (universal screening), is defensible.

## Self-Evaluation of This BrainLift

### Strengths:
1. Every claim traces to specific DOK1 facts with citations and effect sizes.
2. Causal mechanisms are explicit: WHY retesting degrades validity (g-saturation loss), WHY attrition creates bias (differential dropout by SES), WHY appetite signals are confounded (table of confounds per signal).
3. Engages directly with the strongest opposing claims and provides specific rebuttals.
4. The SPOV takes a clear, committed side with no hedging.
5. Cross-domain synthesis: psychometrics + economics + equity research + personality psychology + measurement theory.

### Weaknesses (honest assessment):
1. **The SMPY argument proves too much.** If g predicts everything, why does any non-cognitive factor matter? But we know grit/conscientiousness DO predict at least something (rho = .18). The 12.5:1 ratio is real, but "appetite adds nothing" is too strong.
2. **Universal screening isn't happening.** The policy fix is simple but political will is lacking. Many districts can't or won't fund universal screening. GT Challenge fills a real access gap for families who can't get their child tested at all.
3. **The attrition argument applies to GT Challenge but hasn't been empirically demonstrated.** The claim that multi-session testing introduces SES-correlated attrition is theoretically sound but has no data specific to GT Challenge's design.
4. **The CogAT IS a fixed-form test in a world moving toward adaptive testing.** IRT-adaptive testing is provably more efficient (fewer items for the same precision). This is a legitimate advantage GT Challenge has.
5. **The "coaching = learning" rebuttal is only partly correct.** For format familiarization, Lohman agrees coaching is fine. The question is whether intensive domain-specific practice (months of reasoning training) produces genuine ability growth or just test-specific performance. The answer is probably "some of both" — which partially validates GT Challenge's framing.
